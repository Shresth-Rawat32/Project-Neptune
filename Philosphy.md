## Why This Exists

Project Neptune began with a simple observation: children don't inherently have a complete understanding of the world. They gradually build it by asking questions, connecting new information with what they already know, and correcting their beliefs when they are wrong. This is how they develop a personal understanding of the world, their **"worldview."**

Modern language models are extremely capable, but they primarily rely on knowledge gained from training on predetermined datasets. While they can reason over that knowledge, I became interested in a different question:

> **What happens if an AI learns its worldview the same way a child does?**

It would continuously ask questions, connect information, revise its beliefs, and gradually build an understanding of the world rather than relying solely on what it already knows.

This question led me to educational theories such as constructivism, which describes learning as the process of actively constructing knowledge through experience rather than simply receiving information. Project Neptune does not attempt to reinvent these ideas. Instead, it explores whether similar principles can be applied to artificial intelligence.

Neptune is not a claim that this approach is correct, but rather an exploration driven by curiosity. Whether these ideas succeed or fail, the goal is to better understand what happens when learning, questioning, and long-term understanding become the focus of an AI system rather than simply generating the next response.

##Intelligence is Built Through Architecture

Neptune isn't an attempt to build a better language model, but rather an attempt to explore whether intelligence emerges from the systems surrounding a model rather than the model itself.

Language models will continue to improve each year. Newer models will replace older ones, becoming faster, more knowledgeable, and more capable. If Neptune depended on one specific model, it would eventually become obsolete alongside that model.

Instead, it treats the model as a replaceable component rather than the identity of the project. The project's identity comes from the architecture built around it: persistent memory, active questioning, belief formation, reasoning, and the interactions between these systems.

Rather than asking, "How do we build a smarter model?" Neptune asks, "Can intelligence emerge from a better cognitive architecture?"

If the answer is yes, then replacing the underlying language model should improve Neptune's capabilities without changing what Neptune fundamentally is.

One consequence of this philosophy is that no single component within Neptune is considered "the intelligence." The persistent memory, the question generator, the belief system, even the language model itself—these subsystems working together as part of a bigger system are what Neptune defines as the system's intelligence.

Project Neptune explores the idea that intelligence emerges from the interactions between these subsystems. Each subsystem contributes a different capability, but it is their continuous exchange of information, refinement of beliefs, and reasoning that may produce behavior more intelligent than what only the language model could achieve independently.

We can use the analogy of the human brain as an example. Individual neurons are remarkably simple, yet billions of interconnected neurons give rise to reasoning, memory, creativity, and consciousness. While Neptune doesn't attempt to replicate the brain, it draws inspiration from the broader principle that complex intelligence can emerge from the interaction of many simpler systems rather than from a single all-capable component.

Under this perspective, the language model is not the brain but rather one subsystem within a larger architecture. As new models become available, they can replace older ones without changing Neptune's underlying philosophy. The architecture remains constant while individual components continue to evolve.
